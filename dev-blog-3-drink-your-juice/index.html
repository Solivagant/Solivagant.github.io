DEV BLOG #3 - DRINK YOUR JUICE

24 January 2016 on gunkatana, devblog, gamedev, juice

Juice is that ethereal quality that makes a game pop and go beyond mere interactivity into something that crackles, fizzes, squishes and breathes.

Osman (Mitchell Corporation - arcade - 1996)

Grapefrukt's talk Juice It Or Lose It is the absolute textbook lesson on all things game juice.

I've become fascinated the concept of game juice and I see it now in movies, flashing animations, adverts, TV shows.

It's like seeing The Matrix, and I'm now a really big proponent of it, striving to raise #gamedevs' awareness of Juice and learning when to apply it.

I think of game juice not just as subtly tweening of game elements, but as the various components of animated and interactive content.

I've been collecting the best examples of game juice for my inspiration and entertainment, and I want to share what I've been collecting, hoping to give other #gamedevs the chance to come up with their own juicy creations.

More than just serving as a gorgeous gif gallery, I want to break down the juicy components of each example.

JUICE SAMPLE #1 - R-TYPE LEO

I love this bouncy laser weapon in R-Type Leo, as well as should, cause Gunkatana, the game I'm developing full time, features bouncing lasers.

There's fast flashing red circles, which didn't need to be there, but they increase the intensity of the piece.

When the lasers hit the enemies there's a white red flash and an explosion. On the walls, the bombs create a similar, though smaller, explosive effect, while the lasers firing directly ahead of the ship create a blue and white explosion.

And all of that in such a short gif!

JUICE SAMPLE #2 - TOWERFALL

Towerfall is a great example of a modern indie game that's really in touch with juice.

When the characters jump they're sort of squeezed, their shape is tweened, the same when they're dashing. It's a really interesting way of making the most out of a sprite animation by tweaking it in code and creating a cartoonish animated style.

The arrows have a light on the background, the torches are animated and there's snow in the background, all working together to form a very dynamic level.

That is one of the challenges we are facing developing Gunkatana. As a top down game, we need to think of ways of making the floors and the various elements of the level interesting to look at from that perspective. It's not as easy as just going for a parallax effect.
