Gunkatana is my game in development, a celebration of style and bloodshed. "Easy to learn, hard to master" zero-sum combat with laser guns, swords, and our unique TURBO-RAILS, an ultraviolent take on skateboard grinding.

THE TEAM

So, now that you know what Gunkatana is, who the hell is making it?

Me? I'm Geraldo, a game programmer from Portugal. I moved to the UK almost 4 years ago to follow my passion for these things we call video games, and in 2014 I created a prototype that eventually became Gunkatana.

For this game, I'm inspired by movies like The Matrix and Kill Bill and by games like Hotline Miami, Grand Theft Auto and Towerfall. I relish on satisfying combat mechanics, the weighty feel of attacks and movements, and putting that into the game has been a joy.

The most difficult part for me, but perhaps the most satisfying as well, is the disconnect between what I want to add to the game, and how well what's in my mind translates into the finished project.

Toying around prototyping new combat and movement mechanics is something that really satisfies me, probably more so than adding "content".

The second most difficult part comes from my career development, as I spent most of my time as a software engineer, solving problems, developing an incredibly useful array of skills for game making. But that came at the cost of not developing my creativity, at least in terms of making things and putting them out there for everyone to see (any software engineer / programmer will tell you that it requires a LOT of creativity, and I don't deny it!).

So sometimes I struggle with going on with the project, considering if what I am doing is really worth doing at all, if it is worth bothering other people with. I'm sure everyone feels this as well, at a certain amount.

That said, here we are, Gunkatana is on Steam Greenlight waiting for your votes, we've got a fantastic trailer (with the help of Miriam Ghromann) and our first playable build is out for people to check it out from the comfort of their homes.

Initially a project solely by me and CrazyArcadia, we've since expanded the team:

Sarah Schemske is the project's writer, and she's fleshing out the personalities of our playable characters and the dystopian world where the game is set;

João Nascimento is our producer, which means he makes sure everything is on track, helps us get the game out into the open, and motivates us (it's no wonder we've nicknamed him The Wrath);

Marco Machado, artist, is creating something beautiful that we will be revealing in the coming weeks;

Joonas Turner and Niilo Takalainen, the sound designers behind Broforce, are crafting every katana slash and laser shot spark, and have also given us the first voice of Gunkatana in the form of our sarcastic in-game announcer;

Electric Cafe, the people behind Aqua Kitty and Rock Boshers retro soundtracks, are creating a retrowave soundtrack for the game, that you can already hear in our playable demo and in available samples at their Soundcloud.

I wouldn't be here, working full time on this project, if it wasn't for them, and other friends who have helped us get it this far.

]]><![CDATA[Dev Blog #3 - Drink your Juice]]>https://solivagant.github.io/2016/01/24/dev-blog-3-drink-your-juice/cad22ada-7659-4aba-9ac0-eedc073987f6Sun, 24 Jan 2016 22:10:23 GMT

Juice is that ethereal quality that makes a game pop and go beyond mere interactivity into something that crackles, fizzes, squishes and breathes.

Osman (Mitchell Corporation - arcade - 1996)

Grapefrukt's talk Juice It Or Lose It is the absolute textbook lesson on all things game juice.

I've become fascinated the concept of game juice and I see it now in movies, flashing animations, adverts, TV shows.

It's like seeing The Matrix, and I'm now a really big proponent of it, striving to raise #gamedevs' awareness of Juice and learning when to apply it.

I think of game juice not just as subtly tweening of game elements, but as the various components of animated and interactive content.

I've been collecting the best examples of game juice for my inspiration and entertainment, and I want to share what I've been collecting, hoping to give other #gamedevs the chance to come up with their own juicy creations.

More than just serving as a gorgeous gif gallery, I want to break down the juicy components of each example.

JUICE SAMPLE #1 - R-TYPE LEO

I love this bouncy laser weapon in R-Type Leo, as well as should, cause Gunkatana, the game I'm developing full time, features bouncing lasers.

There's fast flashing red circles, which didn't need to be there, but they increase the intensity of the piece.

When the lasers hit the enemies there's a white red flash and an explosion. On the walls, the bombs create a similar, though smaller, explosive effect, while the lasers firing directly ahead of the ship create a blue and white explosion.

And all of that in such a short gif!

JUICE SAMPLE #2 - TOWERFALL

Towerfall is a great example of a modern indie game that's really in touch with juice.

When the characters jump they're sort of squeezed, their shape is tweened, the same when they're dashing. It's a really interesting way of making the most out of a sprite animation by tweaking it in code and creating a cartoonish animated style.

The arrows have a light on the background, the torches are animated and there's snow in the background, all working together to form a very dynamic level.

That is one of the challenges we are facing developing Gunkatana. As a top down game, we need to think of ways of making the floors and the various elements of the level interesting to look at from that perspective. It's not as easy as just going for a parallax effect.

Since last year I've been having a lot of conversations that start with someone asking me a specific question.

"So what's your game about?"

"Cyberpunk, lasers, katanas, turbo grind rails & lots of juice."

That's how I introduce Gunkatana, the game I'm now developing as a full time indie developer.

Gunkatana was born from a confluence of two events.

BIRTH

The London Game Space hosted an event to showcase locally sourced local multiplayer games.

I had never shown a game to anyone and have them play it and critique it before. I was just starting to get my feet wet developing prototypes in Unity.

I tinkered with a very early top down action game I was working on and tacked a second player on it. Challenged myself to go ahead and show that game at the event.

I developed that first draft of Gunkatana as an attempt to decipher what made Hotline Miami tick.

Watching Hotline Miami's sequel trailer was the second pivotal event. It has inspired me in those initial game dev hours and ever since.

The feedback I got from that initial demo was great. It was already somewhat fun, if incredibly basic and unpolished. Players gave me tons of ideas of where to take it next.

The core gameplay was already there: lasers and turbo speed grind rails.

SPEED KILLS

The speed rails started off as quick way to implement the Matrix's wall running. I wanted to create a game with that sense of speed and action, letting you speed along the walls, Mirror's Edge like.

Eventually I started laying the speed tracks anywhere in the level, and they became speed rails on the floor. The Gunkatana fighters attach their boots to them and zoom along the rails at high speeds, running over whoever's on the way, corpses thrown about and blood flying everywhere.

Sujan, one of the great friends I’ve made at the London Indies scene, told me to actually implement the titular sword from the title. I did so. It's really fun to be writing C# classes called KatanaDashSlash.

FROM NOTHING, EVERYTHING

I needed someone to bring the world of Gunkatana to life, and put out a call for artists on Twitter. A friend, Florian, recommended me one of his friends and thus I met CrazyArcadia.

CrazyArcadia knocked out a beautiful mockup, and I was in love with those pixels. It's always fascinating for me to suddenly see imagination becoming reality.

The core of Gunkatana is a competitive mix of dark futuristic themes and very mechanically tight gameplay, infused with combat from The Matrix and Equilibrium.

Seeing it come to life from my initial programmer art & OpenGameArt assets into something palpable, phosphorous & vibrant, it's an incredible experience.

Both me and CrazyArcadia are responsible for the game design and its a joy to discuss gameplay mechanics and level designs with him.

The result is what you see in this preliminary trailer, featuring original music by Electric Cafe of Aqua Kitty soundtrack fame.

FUTURE

Gunkatana was born from participating in the London indie dev community. The feedback I get when showing it and playing it at events has been invaluable and has helped shape where it's now, and will help shape where it's headed.

Our stand at GameCity 2015, a volunteer driven games event at Nottingham, UK

Gunkatana has received very positive press coverage, at The Guardian and Impact Nottingham.

I'm incredibly proud of what we've achieved so far.

Tying together the efforts of CrazyArcadia, lead artist and game designer on the project, Sarah Schemske, writer for Gunkatana, João Nascimento, website designer, David Anstey, who designed one of our main characters, Nell F., who designed the wonderful poster art, Electric Cafe's music, while simultaneously adding new features, level building techniques, quality of life software engineering practices and just fun stuff like running over your enemies when grinding the rails, makes me incredibly happy.

]]><![CDATA[The Creative Process]]>https://solivagant.github.io/2015/10/30/creativity/e9d1fa25-aa8b-4eaf-9472-fd32d7cbe1c5Fri, 30 Oct 2015 12:00:00 GMTCreativity is reinterpreting what comes inside you into something else.

I struggled for years to come up with something original. That affected my craft, to the point where I didn't consider anything I did original enough; I would stop myself before it started.

I prototyped some ideas, stumbled my way, but when seeing similarities between what I was building and other games, I’d falter, craving to build something wholly original, from the root, and failing to muster the creativity to do so.

That all changed once I read Steal Like an Artist by Austin Kleon while on a flight. I bought it by chance on the airport's shops.

Without access to the internet or a computer, I took notes of what I learned from the book, and my mind was ablaze.

How I approach creativity has completely changed since then.

Artists by education will already have a grasp of this, but for the budding software coder or game designer that is getting in touch with their creativity, the concept of stealing and copying, in order to come up with their own ideas, could seem wrong.

I have involuntarily practiced premature self-rejection when writing and concepting, for years. I'd sabotage myself and wouldn't break through, thinking that comparing what I was making with something else was wrong.

THIS is the message I want to get across: don’t stifle your creative process by outright rejecting your early ideas.

Write down your derivative ideas! Let them percolate in your mind, and then enrich them, mix them, change them, make them your own.

I don’t advocate that it's ok for games like 2048 to spring up, just cloning a game’s mechanics heart and open sourcing it, but it is part of the creative process to learn from other games, take them apart, find out how to build them, and THEN, introduce a secret sauce of your own.

From my experience, that is how creativity works, it’s not a magical faucet that you turn on. Staring at a blank page won’t get you anywhere.

When stuck for ideas, look to your inspirations, reverse engineer them, and then twist them, play with them.

I love making games because the process of making games is so fun and enriching, and because playing games for research IS fun. Mapping other games' features and design, discovering what makes them special, it's how I get my ideas.

Games, books, movies, culture, the world, the way a plane flies in the air, a dewdrop falling from grass, a conversation at the pub, an overheard diatribe, an article, a boring commute, these are all opportunities to take it all in, and plant the seeds that eventually, suddenly, lead to an outpour of ideas.

Exploring the affordance space that you’re creating as you go is like giving birth to a universe of rules and twisting them and changing them as you explore them.

Give your design doc a scrap and turn things upside down: many ideas will look great written down but once played by humans you might realize they don’t work or they need tweaking.

Always be aware that you can change your idea at any time, tweak it, twist it and give it a spin. You could try understanding how other games make it work.

In my opinion, the only wrong thing to do is to craft a direct clone and try to pass it off as original. There’s no creative input, it’s the laziest of all processes: the off-brand copy.

Once you embrace, study, and master your inspirations, whether it’s game mechanics or prose or game genres or potato waffle recipes, you'll just feel free to create, without fearing that its original or not.

It's definitely what happened to me. I proudly embrace the influences of Hotline Miami and Towerfall in the making of Gunkatana, my cyberpunk top down local multiplayer game with single player campaign to boot.

"Nobody ever became a writer just by wanting to be one. If you have anything to say, anything you feel nobody has ever said before, you have got to feel it so desperately that you will find some way to say it that nobody has ever found before, so that the thing you have to say and the way of saying it blend as one matter — as indissolubly as if they were conceived together."

-F. Scott Fitzgerald
